Exercise 0.1

Construct an explicit deformation retraction of the torus with one point deleted onto a graph consisting of two circles intersecting at a point, namely, longitude and meridian circles of the torus.

  • Choose a representation of the torus $\mathbb{T}$. The most straightforward representation of the torus is the quotient of $[-1,1]^2$

Denote the torus by $\mathbb{T}$ defined as the quotient $[-1,1]^2 /\big((-1,t) \sim (1,t) \text{ and } (t,-1) \sim (t,1) \forall t \in [-1,1]\big)$ with quotient map $\rho : [-1,1]^2 \to \mathbb{T}$. With this representation, the longitude and meridian circles of the torus that we choose here are the images of $[-1,1] \times \{0,1\}$ and $\{0,1\} \times [-1,1]$, in no particular order, under $\rho$. These circles intersect once at the image of the corners of $[-1,1]^2$.

Recall that in any normed vector space (over the real or complex numbers), the closed unit ball is convex, and so there is a linear deformation retraction of the punctured closed unit ball to the unit sphere (if punctured in the interior).

Applying this with $\mathbb{R}^2$ equipped with the $\infty$-norm - where, crucially, $\mathbb{D}^{2} = \mathbb{D}_{\infty}^{2} = [-1,1]^2$ - there is a linear deformation retraction given by $$ \begin{align*} H :\,& (\mathbb{D}^{2} - 0) \times I \to \mathbb{D}^{2} - 0 \\[10pt] & (x,t) \mapsto (1-t)x + t\frac{x}{\,\,\lVert x \rVert_{\infty}} \\[10pt] \end{align*} $$ of $\mathbb{D}^{2} - 0$ onto $\mathbb{S}_{\infty}^{1} = \partial \mathbb{D}^2 = (\{0,1\} \times [-1,1]) \cup ([-1,1] \times \{0,1\})$.

Let $\iota : \mathbb{D}^{2} - 0 \to \mathbb{D}^{2}$ be the inclusion map and $p_0 = \rho(0)$. Observe that $(\rho \circ \iota) \circ H$ is constant on the fibers of the quotient map $(\rho \circ \iota) \times \text{id}_{I} : (\mathbb{D}^2 - 0) \times I \to (\mathbb{T} - p_0) \times I$, and so $(\rho \circ \iota) \circ H$ descends to the quotient to a deformation retraction $\widetilde{H} : (\mathbb{T} - p_0) \times I \to \mathbb{T} - p_0$ of $\mathbb{T} - p_0$ onto the longitude and meridan circles of $\mathbb{T}$.
