Exercise 0.2

Construct an explicit deformation retraction of $\mathbb{R}^n - 0$ onto $S^{n - 1}$ for $n > 1$.

Consider the case $n = 2$. Each point $x \in \mathbb{R}^2 - 0$ defines a ray based at the origin in the direction of $x$ that passes through the unit circle. Draw this picture.

Consider the radial projection from the origin $r : \mathbb{R}^n - 0 \to S^{n - 1}$ defined by $r(x) = \frac{x}{\lVert x \rVert}$ where $\lVert \, \cdot \,\rVert$ is the $2$-norm on $\mathbb{R}^n$. Note that $r$ is well-defined since $\lVert x \rVert$ is never zero on $\mathbb{R}^n - 0$. One can see $r$ is continuous by expressing it as a composition of continuous maps.

Given $x \in \mathbb{R}^n - 0$, $r(x)$ is the point on the unit sphere $S^{n - 1}$ where the ray based at the origin in the direciton of $x$ intersects $S^{n - 1}$.

We define a homotopy $H : (\mathbb{R}^n - 0) \times I \to \mathbb{R}^{n} - 0$ from the identity on $\mathbb{R}^n - 0$ to $\iota r$ (where $\iota$ is the inclusion $S^{ n - 1} \hookrightarrow \mathbb{R}^n - 0$) by following this ray towards the point of intersection with $S^{n - 1}$ and show that it is a deformation retraction.

Define $H$ by $$H(-,t) = (1 - t)\text{id}_{\mathbb{R}^n - 0} + t\,\iota r.$$ It is simple to check that $H$ is non-zero, and so is well-defined. $H$ can be written as a composition of multiplication and addition maps, along with the relavent maps above, and so is continuous.

One can now verify that

  • $H(-,0) = \text{id}_{\mathbb{R}^n - 0}$
  • $H(-,1) = \iota r$
  • $H\circ (\iota \times \text{id}_I) = \iota \rho$ where $\rho : S^{n - 1} \times I \to S^{n - 1}$ is the coordinate projection onto $S^{n - 1}$

and so $H$ is indeed a deformation retraction.
