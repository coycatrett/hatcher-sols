Given $x \in \mathbb{R}^{n} - 0$, put $r(x)$ to be the point on the unit sphere $\mathbb{S}^{n - 1}$ where the ray based at the origin in the direciton of $x$ intersects $\mathbb{S}^{n - 1}$, as indicated below.

The map $r : \mathbb{R}^{n} - 0 \to \mathbb{S}^{n - 1}$ above is the radial projection from the origin onto $\mathbb{S}^{n - 1}$ and has explicit formula given by $r(x) = \frac{x}{\lVert x \rVert}$ where $\lVert \, \cdot \,\rVert$ is the usual $2$-norm on $\mathbb{R}^{n}$. Note that $r$ is well-defined since $\lVert x \rVert$ is never zero on $\mathbb{R}^{n} - 0$. One can see that $r$ is continuous since it is the composition of continuous maps.

We define a homotopy $H : (\mathbb{R}^{n} - 0) \times I \to \mathbb{R}^{n} - 0$ from the identity on $\mathbb{R}^{n} - 0$ to $\iota r$ (where $\iota$ is the inclusion $\mathbb{S}^{n - 1} \hookrightarrow \mathbb{R}^{n} - 0$) by following this ray towards its point of intersection with $\mathbb{S}^{n - 1}$ and show that it is a deformation retraction.

Define $H$ by $$H(-,t) = (1 - t)\text{id}_{\mathbb{R}^{n} - 0} + t\,\iota r.$$ Since $H(x,-)$ parametrizes the line segment from $x$ to $r(x)$ for each $x$ (and so is never $0$), then $H$ is well-defined. $H$ can be written as a composition of continuous functions, and is therefore continuous.

One can now verify that

  • $H(x,0) = x$ for all $x \in \mathbb{R}^{n} - 0$
  • $H(x,1) = \iota r(x) \in \mathbb{S}^{n - 1}$ for all $x \in \mathbb{R}^{n} - 0$
  • $H(x,t) = x$ for all $(x,t) \in \mathbb{S}^{n - 1} \times I$

and so $H$ is indeed a deformation retraction.
