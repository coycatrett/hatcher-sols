Denote the torus by $\mathbb{T}$, defined as the quotient $[-1,1]^{2} /\big((-1,t) \sim (1,t) \text{ and } (t,-1) \sim (t,1) \forall t \in [-1,1]\big)$ with quotient map $\rho : [-1,1]^{2} \to \mathbb{T}$. With this representation, the longitude and meridian circles of the torus that we choose here are the images of $[-1,1] \times \{0,1\}$ and $\{0,1\} \times [-1,1]$, in no particular order, under $\rho$. These circles intersect once at the image of the corners of $[-1,1]^{2}$ and correspond to the opposite pairs of edges in $\partial [-1,1]^{2}$.

Given $x \in [-1,1]^{2}- 0$, put $r(x)$ to be the point on $\partial [-1,1]^{2}$ where the ray based at the origin in the direction of $x$ intersects $\partial [-1,1]^{2}$.

The map $r : [-1,1]^{2} - 0 \to \partial [-1,1]^{2}$ above is the radial projection from the origin onto $\partial [-1,1]^{2}$ and has explicit formula given by $r(x) = \frac{x}{\,\,\lVert x \rVert_{\infty}}$, where $\lVert \, \cdot \, \rVert_{\infty}$ is the $\infty$-norm.

The significance of the $\infty$-norm above is that $\mathbb{D}^{2}_{\infty} = [-1,1]^{2}$ and $\mathbb{S}^{1}_{\infty} = \partial[-1,1]^{2}$, so this is simply an instance of the radial projection from the origin to the unit circle in a normed vector space.

For the reasons above, we will refer to $[-1,1]^{2}$ as $\mathbb{D}^{2}$ and $\partial [-1,1]^{2}$ as $\mathbb{S}^{1}$ for the remainder of the solution.

We define a homotopy $H : (\mathbb{D}^{2} - 0) \times I \to \mathbb{D}^{2} - 0$ from the identity on $\mathbb{D}^{2} - 0$ to $\iota r$ (where $\iota$ is the inclusion $\mathbb{S}^{1} \hookrightarrow \mathbb{D}^{2} - 0$) by having each point follow the ray it determines to its point of intersection with $\mathbb{S}^{1}$ and show that it is a deformation retraction onto $\mathbb{S}^{1}$. Then we show that $H$ descends to a deformation retraction $\widetilde{H} : (\mathbb{T} - 0) \times I \to \mathbb{T} - 0$ onto the longitude and meridian circles of $\mathbb{T}$ that we have chosen, i.e., the image of $\mathbb{S}^{1}$ under $\rho$.

To that end, define $H$ by $$H(-,t) = (1 - t)\text{id}_{\mathbb{D}^{2} - 0} + t\,\iota r.$$ Since $H(x,-)$ parametrizes the line segment from $x$ to $r(x)$ for each $x$ (and is therefore never $0$), $H$ is well-defined. $H$ may be written as a composition of continuous functions, and is therefore continuous.

One can now verify that

  • $H(x,0) = x$ for all $x \in \mathbb{D}^{2} - 0$
  • $H(x,1) = \iota r(x) \in \mathbb{S}^{1}$ for all $x \in \mathbb{D}^{2} - 0$
  • $H(x,t) = x$ for all $(x,t) \in \mathbb{S}^{1} \times I$

and so $H$ is a deformation retraction onto $\mathbb{S}^{1}$.

Let $p_{0} = \rho(0)$ and $\rho_{0} : \mathbb{D}^{2} - 0 \to \mathbb{T} - p_{0}$ be the restriction of $\rho$ to $\mathbb{D}^{2} - 0$. Notice that $\rho_{0} \circ H$ is constant on the fibers of the quotient map $\rho_{0} \times \text{id}_{I} : (\mathbb{D}^{2} - 0) \times I \to (\mathbb{T} - p_{0}) \times I$, and so $\rho_{0} \circ H$ descends to the quotient to a homotopy $\widetilde{H} : (\mathbb{T} - p_{0}) \times I \to \mathbb{T} - p_{0}$.

  • $\widetilde{H}(\rho_{0}(x),1) = \rho_{0}(\iota r(x)) \in \rho_{0}(\mathbb{S}^{1})$ for all $x \in \mathbb{D}^{2} - 0$
  • $\widetilde{H}(\rho_{0}(x),t) = \rho_{0}(x)$ for all $(x,t) \in \mathbb{S}^{1} \times I$

and so $\widetilde{H}$ is a deformation retraction onto $\rho_{0}(\mathbb{S}^{1})$, i.e., the union of the longitude and meridian circles in $\mathbb{T}$.

One can then compute that $\widetilde{H}$ has explicit formula $$\widetilde{H}(\rho_{0}(x), t) = \rho_{0} \left((1-t)x + t\frac{x}{\,\,\lVert x \rVert_{\infty}}\right).$$ Obtaining formulae for various representations of $\mathbb{T}$ from this one is easier now, as we need only pass through a homeomorphism.
